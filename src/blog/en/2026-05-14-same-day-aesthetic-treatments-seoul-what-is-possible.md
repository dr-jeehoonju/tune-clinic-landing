## The patient who has a flight at 8 p.m.

The message arrives in a familiar form. The patient has already booked their ticket. They land at Incheon mid-morning, they have a hotel for a few hours, and they would like to have something done before their evening flight home. Is that possible?

Sometimes the answer is yes. Sometimes it is a qualified yes — possible if the right treatment is chosen and the consultation confirms it is appropriate. Sometimes it is no, and the reason is not bureaucratic; the reason is that the body needs time that the itinerary has not allocated for it.

This article is an attempt to give that answer some structure, so that the conversation with your physician begins with shared expectations rather than negotiation at the door.

## What does "same-day" actually mean?

In this context, same-day means the full sequence — arrival in Seoul, consultation, treatment, and discharge — completed within a single calendar day, typically followed by travel that day or the following morning.

It is worth stating plainly what that sequence requires. A consultation must happen before any treatment. Not as a formality, not as a brief exchange of pleasantries before the procedure begins, but as a genuine clinical encounter: the physician reviews your history, examines your face, identifies any contraindications, and designs a plan specific to you. Informed consent follows — also before treatment, also not abbreviated.

This is not a local regulation. It is the minimum that makes any treatment medically defensible. Same-day treatment is possible at Tune Clinic precisely because the consultation comes first and shapes what happens next — not because patients can walk in with a fixed plan and have it executed on the spot.

The implication: same-day visits require some time. A realistic estimate, including consultation, examination, treatment, and a recovery window, is four to six hours. Patients who arrive assuming the procedure itself will be the longest part of the day are usually correct — but they sometimes underestimate everything that properly surrounds it.

## Which treatments are commonly same-day eligible?

A handful of treatments can be completed and tolerated within a same-day framework, subject to physician assessment.

**Masseter Botox** — the most same-day-friendly treatment in the aesthetic repertoire. The injection itself is brief. Post-treatment requirements are minimal: upright for four hours, no intense heat, no significant pressure on the jaw. Most patients are comfortable on a flight the same evening. The clinical caveat is that first-time masseter treatment occasionally produces a transient ache in the joint area over the first few days; patients should be aware of this before they travel.

**Skin Botox (microinjection technique)** — the same general permissiveness applies. Multiple small intradermal injections produce temporary papules that resolve within a few hours. Same-day travel is generally well-tolerated.

**Light Rejuran session** — Rejuran PDRN microinjections for skin quality, in a moderate volume and without extensive add-ons, are within the same-day window for most patients. The immediate post-treatment appearance includes small injection-site papules and mild redness, typically resolving within 12–24 hours. The caveat: same-day Rejuran is reasonable if the patient has had it before and knows how their skin responds. A first session, with an unknown individual response, is a higher-risk choice before a long-haul flight.

**Selected pico laser sessions** — low-fluence toning sessions without significant resurfacing can be done same-day for many patients. The skin will be slightly flushed. There is no open wound, no meaningful barrier disruption. The caveat is that more aggressive parameters — enough to produce visible peeling or crusting — belong in a different timeline entirely.

**Low-volume HA filler in low-risk zones** — cheeks, chin, lateral face — can be same-day eligible in the right patient and the right indication. The volume matters; a single syringe in a well-chosen location is a different proposition from bilateral three-syringe augmentation. The zone matters even more. Low-risk zones are anatomically forgiving in a way that glabella, nose, and tear trough are not.

## Which treatments usually need at least an overnight gap?

Some treatments are categorically outside the same-day window. Others are conditional but require more buffer than a single day provides.

**Ultherapy and Thermage** are both sessions of meaningful duration — a full-face Ultherapy treatment takes 60 to 90 minutes, sometimes longer. Beyond the time commitment, both produce post-procedure swelling and tenderness that benefit from a night's observation before travel. Flying the day of an Ultherapy session is asking tissue that has just been mechanically stressed to adapt to cabin pressure and dehydration simultaneously. The next-day departure is the minimum; two days is more defensible.

**High-risk zone filler** — glabella, nose, tear trough, temple — is technically performable same-day, but it requires something the timeline must accommodate: additional post-treatment monitoring time before the patient leaves the building. The rare but serious complication of [vascular occlusion](/blog/filler-vascular-occlusion-explained.html) has its highest-risk window in the first hours after injection. In high-risk anatomical zones, this means the patient should not be on a plane within two to four hours of treatment. If the same-day flight is several hours away and the physician is satisfied with the assessment, high-risk zone filler can be done — but the decision belongs to the physician, made at consultation, not preset.

**Multi-step combination protocols** — any design that stacks a device treatment with injectables, or multiple injectable categories — produce a cumulative acute response that makes same-day travel genuinely inadvisable. The [trip-planning post](/blog/plan-aesthetic-treatments-short-seoul-trip.html) covers this in detail: the problem with combination protocols on a short timeline is not any single treatment, but the additive recovery load.

**Anything requiring a patch test** — a small number of treatments (certain topical anesthetic formulations, some collagen-stimulating products, any product the physician has not used with this patient before) require a test application in advance. This is rare in the treatments that dominate international patient requests, but if it applies, same-day is simply not available.

## Why might a physician decline same-day even when the patient asks for it?

This is worth explaining because patients sometimes experience a declined same-day request as an obstacle. It is the opposite.

**Incomplete medical history.** A consultation that reveals blood thinner use — prescription anticoagulants, but also regular ibuprofen, high-dose fish oil, ginkgo — changes what is safe. Some situations require a washout period before treatment. That cannot be compressed into a same-day visit.

**Recent illness or significant physiological change.** A patient who traveled 12 hours and is fighting a mild infection, or whose skin barrier is compromised from a flight, is not in the same physiological state as a rested patient at home. The physician may want to see you on a second day when baseline is more clearly established.

**Anatomical complexity that needs more thought.** A patient with prior filler from another clinic, visible asymmetry, or structural features that make the injection plan genuinely more involved may be better served by a consultation today and a treatment at a return visit. This is not a business decision. It is a clinical one.

**Unrealistic expectations identified in consultation.** If the consultation reveals a significant gap between what the patient expects and what the treatment can deliver — in a single same-day session — the responsible course is to have that conversation fully, even if it means no treatment occurs on that visit.

**The physician is not satisfied.** That is, in some ways, the whole answer. The same-day decision is the physician's after consultation, not the patient's at booking. A clinic that allows the patient to pre-authorize same-day treatment without seeing them is reversing the process that makes same-day treatment safe.

## What does a same-day visit actually look like, practically?

The logistics matter and are worth making explicit.

**Morning arrival works better than afternoon.** If you land at 10 a.m. and have a consultation at noon, treatment at 1 p.m., and a 90-minute recovery window, you are looking at a 3:00–3:30 p.m. departure from the clinic — which is workable for an evening flight. An afternoon arrival compresses every part of this.

**The consultation is not brief.** Plan for 45 minutes to an hour. Photos are taken, your history is reviewed in detail, your face is examined, the plan is discussed, and informed consent is completed. Patients who arrive expecting a five-minute check before treatment are regularly surprised, and that surprise is a feature of a clinic doing the process correctly.

**Recovery windows are treatment-dependent.** After most Botox, you can leave fairly promptly. After light Rejuran, an hour of observation is typical. After filler in any zone, including low-risk areas, we prefer to see you settled before you go. Do not schedule an airport taxi for immediately after the appointment.

**The flight interval matters, not just the day.** Same-day is not a binary question — "did treatment and flight happen on the same calendar day?" What matters is the hours between end of treatment and boarding. For Botox, four hours is the baseline. For filler of any kind, including low-risk zones, we recommend a minimum of four to six hours before flight. For high-risk zone filler, the minimum stretches further, and the physician will advise specifically based on what was done.

## What should I do before arriving — and what should I avoid?

The preparation before a same-day visit is more important than for a scheduled visit with recovery time, because there is no buffer.

**Bring a current medications list.** Everything — prescriptions, supplements, anything you take regularly. Blood thinners of any kind (prescription anticoagulants, NSAIDs like ibuprofen, aspirin at doses above 75 mg, fish oil at high doses, vitamin E, ginkgo biloba) should ideally be stopped five to seven days before treatment. If this was not communicated in advance and you have been taking them, tell the physician in consultation. They need to know.

**Bring recent photos of yourself** — from the past few months, in natural light, front and profile. These are part of the clinical documentation and help the physician understand your baseline and recent changes.

**No makeup if possible.** The examination and photo documentation are more useful without it. If you arrive in full coverage, allow time to remove it before the consultation.

**No aesthetic procedures from other clinics in the recent window.** Filler, Botox, lasers, or any injectable treatment performed in the weeks before your visit at another clinic changes the clinical picture. Disclose everything. A same-day plan based on incomplete disclosure is a same-day plan built on incomplete information.

**Be rested.** This sounds obvious. It is sometimes not. A patient who has been traveling for 14 hours, has not slept, and is acutely dehydrated is not presenting the same skin barrier or physiological state as a rested patient. If the visit matters to you, build in the time to arrive in reasonable condition.

## What about the flight after same-day treatment?

The short version: treatment type determines the minimum pre-flight window, not just the day.

After Botox, four hours upright post-injection is the standard recommendation; most patients tolerate same-day flights well after this window. After skin Botox or light Rejuran, the same general principle applies with the addition that the skin barrier is mildly compromised — keep it hydrated through the flight.

After filler — even low-risk zone, low-volume filler — the minimum we recommend before boarding is four to six hours. This allows the early acute response to declare itself clearly. The rare but real warning signs of [vascular occlusion](/blog/filler-vascular-occlusion-explained.html) are most relevant in this window, and being on a plane with them unrecognized is the scenario worth preventing. After any filler in high-risk anatomical zones, the physician will specify the interval; as a general guide, no same-day flight after glabella, nose, or tear trough filler.

After Ultherapy, Thermage, or any energy-based lifting device: the next day at the earliest, and the physician may recommend longer depending on the acute response.

## The consultation-first principle — why same-day is possible at all

The reason same-day treatment is clinically defensible — when it is — is not because the treatments are fast. It is because the consultation comes first and shapes everything after it.

Patients sometimes arrive with a plan they have assembled from research: I want X and Y done, I have read about them, I know what they involve. That research is useful context. It is not a prescription. The consultation is the moment when that context meets clinical reality: your anatomy, your history, your skin on this day, and the physician's judgment about what is appropriate in the time available.

This principle — that the protocol is designed in the consultation room, not pre-purchased online — is the foundation of the [Tune Clinic design method](/design-method.html). It is what separates a same-day visit from a same-day transaction. The former is safe when the clinical conditions support it. The latter is not a medical concept.

For patients who want to maximize the efficiency of a same-day or short visit, the most useful thing to do before arriving is a remote pre-consultation. Sharing photographs, going through history, and discussing goals with the physician before you land means the in-person consultation can move more quickly to examination and confirmation rather than starting from zero. This is available at [consult.html](/consult.html) and is particularly relevant for patients with a fixed travel itinerary.

The [short Seoul trip planning post](/blog/plan-aesthetic-treatments-short-seoul-trip.html) covers the broader question of how to sequence treatments across longer windows — 24 hours, 48 hours, three days, seven days — and is a useful companion to the same-day question if you have any flexibility in your schedule.

## Is same-day right for you?

The honest answer is: we don't know until we see you.

For some patients — returning visitors with an established physician relationship, straightforward cases, low-risk treatment choices, adequate pre-flight windows — same-day is reasonable, safe, and a sensible use of a limited trip. For others, the consultation will reveal something that changes the plan: a medication that needs a washout, an anatomical finding that deserves more thought, a realistic discussion about what one treatment can achieve that takes longer than expected.

Same-day is a feature of physician judgment and clinic preparedness working together. It is not an unconditional offering, and a clinic that presents it as one is not a clinic whose judgment you should trust for the rest of the process. The visit that ends with "we recommend you return on a longer trip for this" is not a failed visit. It is often the most useful one you can have.

For patients who want to begin the process before booking flights, [remote pre-consultation](/consult.html) is the appropriate first step.
