Pico Fractional Laser — How Ultra-Short Pulses Rebuild Skin Without Burning It

A physician explains how pico fractional lasers use picosecond pulses to create tiny bubbles inside the dermis — stimulating collagen, refining pores, and softening scars without the downtime of ablative resurfacing.

Dr. Seung Yeon Cha

Medical Director

Dr. Jee Hoon Ju

Aesthetic Medicine Physician

The patient who had tried everything

She sat down in the consultation room and opened her phone. Scroll, scroll, scroll — a long album of photographs, each one taken in the same bathroom light, each one a few months apart. Enlarged pores across the cheeks. A dusting of old acne scars near the jawline. The kind of texture you only really see up close, in a mirror, at the end of a long day.

“I’ve done chemical peels. I’ve done microneedling. I’ve done a CO₂ laser once — the downtime was brutal and I’m not sure it did much. I don’t want to disappear for two weeks again. But I also don’t want another treatment that just feels nice and changes nothing.”

This is the most common conversation we have about skin texture. Not with patients who are new to aesthetic medicine — with patients who have already tried most of the obvious things. They are not looking for magic. They are looking for a treatment with a real mechanism, without a real recovery.

That is exactly the gap pico fractional lasers were designed to fill.

What “pico” actually means — and why it matters

The word pico refers to the pulse duration: one picosecond is 10⁻¹² seconds. Older Q-switched lasers operate in the nanosecond range (10⁻⁹). That sounds like a small difference until you realize a picosecond pulse is a thousand times shorter than a nanosecond pulse.

That shortening changes the physics of what happens inside the skin.

  • Nanosecond lasers work primarily through photothermal effects — they heat their target until it breaks down.
  • Picosecond lasers deliver energy so quickly that heat has no time to diffuse. Instead, the dominant mechanism becomes photomechanical / photoacoustic — energy arrives as a pressure wave rather than as sustained heat.

This matters for one reason: less heat means less collateral damage, and less collateral damage means lower risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation — a particularly important consideration in Asian skin.

LIOB — the tiny bubbles that rebuild the skin

Here is where the fractional mode becomes interesting. A diffractive lens array (often called an MLA, or micro-lens array) splits a single pico pulse into 100–200 microbeams, each concentrating energy into a narrow focal point inside the dermis.

When that focal intensity exceeds a specific threshold, something called LIOB (Laser-Induced Optical Breakdown) occurs. Briefly, ionization inside the tissue creates a tiny cavitation bubble — a microscopic pocket of vaporized dermal content — without injuring the overlying epidermis.

This is the core mechanism: controlled microtrauma deep in the dermis, with a functionally intact skin surface above it.

Each of those bubbles is a biological signal. The fibroblasts around them sense injury, switch into repair mode, and begin laying down new collagen and elastin. Over the following weeks, the extracellular matrix reorganizes, dermal density increases, and the skin above tightens from below — without ever being burned off.

In other words: you get the remodeling response of a fractional laser without the resurfacing cost.

What the skin actually does over time

Most patients want to know what happens and when. The histologic timeline is reasonably consistent:

Timeframe What is happening in the skin What the patient notices
Immediate Vertical cavitation bubbles in the upper-to-mid dermis; no epidermal ablation Mild pinkness, brief warmth; makeup often possible the next day
1–4 weeks Type I and III collagen upregulation, elastin remodeling, ECM reorganization Smoother texture, early glow
8–12+ weeks Dermal thickening, pore tightening, scar remodeling, tone homogenization Visible refinement of pores and shallow scars; more even tone

This is why a single session is rarely the answer. Dermal remodeling is incremental, and the most meaningful changes appear across a series of 5–10 sessions, spaced 2–4 weeks apart.

What it is good at — and what it is not

Pico fractional is a strong tool, but it is not the right answer for every problem. Being clear about this saves patients from disappointment.

Where pico fractional performs well

  • Enlarged pores — as dermal density increases, pore circumference decreases
  • Rough texture and early photoaging — a smoother dermal-epidermal interface improves light scattering and “glow”
  • Mild-to-moderate atrophic acne scars, especially rolling scars
  • Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) and dull, uneven tone
  • Melasma — but only as an adjunct, with very cautious parameters
  • “Glass skin” biorejuvenation as part of a broader protocol

Where it falls short

  • Icepick scars — too narrow and deep for LIOB alone to remodel meaningfully
  • Deep boxcar scars — generally require ablative resurfacing (Er:YAG or CO₂ fractional) or combination approaches
  • Aggressive melasma with high fluence settings — can paradoxically worsen the pigmentation

Pico fractional is not a substitute for genuine ablative resurfacing when the problem is deep structural scarring. Pretending otherwise leads to the same frustration that brought the patient in the first place.

How we choose parameters for Asian skin

Device-specific numbers vary, but the underlying logic does not. For Korean and broader Asian skin types, we work within a fairly narrow corridor:

  • Wavelength: 1064 nm (Nd:YAG picosecond) is the workhorse — the safest choice for darker skin types because of its lower melanin absorption. 755 nm (Alexandrite) can be used for stubborn pigment but carries a higher PIH risk. 532 nm is reserved for very superficial lesions and used with caution.
  • Spot size: 6–10 mm
  • Fluence (energy density): 0.2–0.8 J/cm² — intentionally modest
  • Repetition rate: 5–10 Hz
  • Passes: 1–3, depending on indication and patient tolerance
  • Strategy: high density, low fluence

That last line — high density, low fluence — is the single most important principle for treating Asian skin safely. It produces enough microinjuries to drive remodeling without pushing thermal load into the range where PIH becomes likely. It is less dramatic per session, but far more predictable across a series.

What the day after looks like

This is the part most patients actually care about, especially those traveling to Seoul on a compressed schedule.

  • Pain during treatment: mild to moderate — noticeably less than CO₂ or Erbium fractional
  • Erythema: typically 3–24 hours, occasionally into the next morning
  • Swelling: minimal
  • Makeup: often possible the same evening, almost always by the next day
  • Aftercare: immediate cooling, a bland moisturizer, and strict sun avoidance; vitamin C or exosomes can support recovery and amplify the collagen response

The downtime profile is the reason patients who rejected ablative resurfacing are willing to commit to a series. A treatment you can actually fit into your life is a treatment you will actually finish.

Where it fits compared with other fractional lasers

Feature Pico Fractional Erbium Fractional CO₂ Fractional
Mechanism LIOB (non-ablative) Ablative Deep ablative & coagulative
Downtime Minimal Moderate High
PIH risk Low Medium High
Scar efficacy Mild–moderate Moderate Strong
Safety in Asian skin Excellent Good Caution required

Pico fractional is not trying to be CO₂. It is trying to be the treatment you can repeat, refine, and layer into a broader plan without derailing your week.

Where the real results come from — combinations

In clinical practice, pico fractional rarely performs alone. Its most consistent results come from thoughtful combinations with other modalities:

  • Pico fractional + skin boosters — pore tightening plus hydration; the booster is usually scheduled 1–2 weeks after the pico session
  • Pico fractional + LDM (low-level ultrasound) — reduces post-procedure inflammation and can be done the same day
  • Pico fractional + RF microneedling — the strongest combination for moderate atrophic acne scars, pairing dermal LIOB with deeper thermal remodeling
  • Pico fractional + subcision — for tethered rolling scars, where mechanical release unlocks what the laser alone cannot reach
  • Pico fractional + Sylfirm X — for pigmentation with a vascular component, such as vascular-type melasma

The common theme: pico fractional is excellent at texture and dermal density, but it is most powerful when paired with something that addresses the other half of the problem.

A few pearls worth remembering

  1. High density, low fluence is the safest and most effective strategy for Asian skin. If a protocol pushes aggressive fluence to chase a faster result, that is the first red flag.
  2. Melasma must be handled with restraint. Excess fluence is the most reliable way to make it worse, not better.
  3. Pico fractional is not ablative resurfacing. Deep icepick or boxcar scars need an ablative partner.
  4. Pores don’t respond in one session. Dermal remodeling is incremental — the series is the treatment.
  5. For acne scars, combinations beat monotherapy. Subcision + pico, or RF microneedling + pico, consistently outperforms pico alone.

What this means for patients considering pico fractional

Back to the patient with the phone full of photographs. The question was never “which device is best?” It was “which approach has a real mechanism, produces real change, and respects my life outside the clinic?”

Pico fractional answers that question well — for the right indications, with the right parameters, and inside a plan that acknowledges what it cannot do alone. The machine matters less than the framework it is used within: correct wavelength for the skin type, disciplined fluence, realistic session count, and an honest conversation about where combinations will be necessary.

When those variables line up, patients stop taking those scrolling bathroom photographs. Not because their skin has become something dramatic — but because, finally, there is nothing they feel the need to document.
